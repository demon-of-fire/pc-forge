# What is TDP?

**TDP** stands for **Thermal Design Power**. It is a measurement expressed in Watts (W) that indicates the maximum amount of heat a computer chip (usually a CPU or GPU) is expected to output under a theoretical load.

## Is TDP the same as Power Consumption?

Not exactly. While they are related, they aren't the same:

1. **TDP** is a target for the *cooling system*. It tells the cooler manufacturer: "This chip can put out 125W of heat; make sure your cooler can dissipate that much."
2. **Power Consumption** is the actual amount of electricity the chip draws from the PSU. Modern CPUs often have "Boost" or "Turbo" modes that allow them to exceed their TDP for short periods, drawing significantly more power.

## Why does TDP matter?

- **Cooler Choice:** If your CPU has a TDP of 125W, using a basic stock cooler might lead to "thermal throttling," where the CPU slows itself down to avoid overheating.
- **Case Airflow:** High TDP components generate more heat, meaning you need better fans and airflow to keep the rest of your components cool.
- **PSU Sizing:** While not the only factor, higher TDP usually correlates with higher power draw.

## Quick Guide to TDP

- **65W:** Energy efficient, usually runs cool. Great for small form factor (SFF) builds.
- **105W - 150W:** Standard for high-performance gaming CPUs. Requires a decent air cooler or a 240mm AIO.
- **200W+:** Extreme performance. Requires high-end 360mm AIOs or massive dual-tower air coolers.
